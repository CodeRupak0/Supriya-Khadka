Title: My Experience as a Polling Officer in the Election 2082
Date: 2082 Falgun 21st
Author: — Supriya Khadka

Content:
The events of Bhadra 23 and 24, marked by the massive Gen-Z protests, created a significant shake-up in Nepal’s political landscape. In the midst of that uncertainty, former Chief Justice Sushila Karki stepped forward to lead the country as Prime Minister of the interim government.
She formed a team of interim ministers to manage different sectors and ensure that the government continued to function despite the unstable situation. The primary goal of the interim government was to maintain stability, carry out essential state functions, and most importantly, pave the way for the formation of a new permanent government through timely elections.
However, the interim government faced considerable criticism and skepticism. Many questioned its legitimacy and doubted whether it would be able to fulfill the demands raised during the Gen-Z protests. Some even claimed that the government would fail to conduct the elections on time and that the scheduled election date of 21st Falgun 2082 would have to be postponed. Despite these doubts, the interim government, under the leadership of Prime Minister Sushila Karki, proved the critics wrong by successfully conducting the elections on the scheduled date in a peaceful environment. For that, I feel truly grateful and appreciative of her leadership and commitment.
For me personally, the election was a very special experience because it was the first time I had the opportunity to serve the nation as a Polling Officer. I had only recently become an officer, and this responsibility felt both exciting and challenging. I was assigned to Janata Secondary School, English Medium, Itahari-09, Area ‘Ga’. Interestingly, it was also the first time in my life that I got to vote myself, and I voted under the Proportional Representation system. Experiencing both roles as an election official and a voter which made the moment even more meaningful. Among all the staff members, I was the youngest, yet I had the responsibility of leading the team. Our group consisted of a Polling Officer, an Assistant Polling Officer, assistant staff, a helper, and three volunteers with a total of nine members. On the 18th of Falgun, we collected the election materials and booth from the Main Election Officer’s Office and headed directly to our assigned polling location.
We stayed at the polling site, which was inside a classroom of Janata Secondary School. All five polling officers guarded the booth day and night and spent three nights there i.e. from the 18th to the 20th of Falgun. The stay was not easy since we had limited bedding and basic resources, but everyone remained committed to their responsibilities. On the morning of 21st Falgun, we woke up at 4 a.m. and began preparing for the voting process. After completing all the necessary arrangements, voting officially began at 7 a.m. Soon after, voters started arriving. From elderly citizens to young voters, the polling booth remained busy throughout the day. Even though we worked the entire day on empty stomachs, we carried out our duties with full dedication and responsibility, ensuring that the voting process remained smooth and peaceful.
Voting was scheduled to end at 5 p.m., but those who had already entered the polling area were allowed to cast their votes. The final vote was cast at around 5:05 p.m., marking the official end of the voting process. After that, we carefully sealed the ballot boxes, prepared the necessary documents, and signed all required papers. Once everything was completed, most staff members left the polling site, while we the polling officers waited for the bus that would transport the ballot boxes safely to the Main Election Officer’s Office. The bus arrived around 7 p.m., accompanied by police personnel and security from the Armed Police Force who were responsible for safeguarding the ballot boxes. We then
traveled back and successfully submitted the ballot boxes and election materials. The following day was the vote-counting day, and I also had the opportunity to participate in the counting process. Overall, this was a truly unforgettable experience for me. Not only did I get the chance to serve as a Polling Officer and exercise my right to vote for the first time, but I was also able to witness Nepal’s democratic process from very close. As a young woman entrusted with the responsibility of managing staff, coordinating with security personnel, and ensuring the smooth operation of a polling station, the task was certainly demanding. Yet, despite the exhaustion, it was an incredibly rewarding and proud moment in my life.
